Entrepreneurial Co-op - Autonomous Solar Panel Cleaning & Cooling Solution

Mentored by: Dr. Andrew Rowe, Acting Director, Institute for Integrated Energy Systems

Overview

Soiling and overheating cause major solar efficiency losses—up to 30% annual reduction from dirt accumulation and ~0.5% efficiency loss per °C of overheating.

This project developed an automated cleaning and cooling system to recover lost efficiency, minimize operational costs, and shorten payback periods. The system integrates gravity-fed daytime operation, TOU-optimized refilling, and modular retrofitting for residential rooftops, targeting deployment in hot, arid regions like Los Angeles.

System Architecture

  • Gravity-fed rooftop reservoir: Positioned above the panels, preloaded during low Time-of-Use (TOU) electricity periods to enable daytime cleaning and cooling without active pumping.
  • Facade-integrated design: The elevated tank is enclosed within a chimney-style façade to maintain residential aesthetics while supporting functional performance.
  • SolidWorks structural modeling: Captured full integration with two-story, angled-roof homes, ensuring realistic retrofitting and structural feasibility.
  • Water-efficient operation: Cleaning cycles use precisely scheduled, controlled volumes to minimize waste while maintaining effective panel coverage.
  • Shared piping network: A common upper reservoir supplies both cleaning and cooling through a simplified valve and piping system to optimize material use.

Control System

  • Microcontroller-based automation: Coordinates pump scheduling, valve sequencing, and reservoir monitoring for efficient system operation.
  • TOU-aware control algorithms: Prioritize off-peak pumping and gravity-driven daytime operation to minimize energy costs.
  • Adaptive cleaning schedules: Adjust cleaning frequency seasonally based on soiling rates for water conservation and maximum performance.
  • Delay-based filtration: Drains the dirtiest initial runoff separately before filtering and returning water to the lower reservoir, extending filter life.
  • Built-in reliability features: Include dry-run pump protection, low-reservoir alarms, and an optional rapid-drain function to empty the overhead tank during high-wind events to protect rooftop structures.

Modeling & Performance Validation

  • Thermal simulation: Built MATLAB models solving non-linear radiative and convective heat balances via Newton-Raphson iteration to predict panel temperatures.
  • Annual system modeling: Simulated 8760 hours of operation using Los Angeles NSRDB weather data for a 5 kW rooftop system, modeling cooling impact, reservoir heating, and seasonal performance.
  • Pump and flow optimization: Applied Darcy-Weisbach modeling to right-size pumps for daily operation without oversizing for rare extreme conditions.
  • Reservoir thermal behavior: Modeled daytime water heating to optimize cleaning and cooling schedules based on evolving system efficiency.

Cost and Economic Analysis

  • Material cost estimate: USD ~$2,870–$4,205, depending on system configuration.
  • Annual electricity savings: Up to USD ~$785 compared to unmanaged arrays.
  • Discounted payback periods:
    • 5.86 years (cleaning and cooling)
    • 7.07 years (cleaning only)
    • 9.68 years (no intervention)

Key Outcomes

  • Recovered ~40% of lost solar efficiency through integrated cleaning and cooling.
  • Achieved low operational energy use (~0.025 kWh/day) via passive flow and TOU-based optimization.
  • Delivered a modular, scalable system adaptable to different rooftops and climates.
  • Validated system feasibility and return on investment through full-year thermal, flow, and cost modeling.
  • Future refinement will focus on heat exchanger optimization to further enhance cooling performance.

Recirculating Water Bath - Capstone Project

Recipient of the Marianne Black Award for Best Design


Background

Built for Dr. Sravya Tekumalla’s research lab, this custom water bath was designed to support high-precision thermal testing in biomedical applications involving advanced metals and additive manufacturing.

Objectives

  • Maintain temperatures from ambient to 60°C for up to 2 months
  • Hold 16–22 test tubes with custom stainless steel trays
  • Ensure ±1°C thermal stability
  • Prioritize user-friendliness, safety, and low energy consumption

Engineering Approach

  • Arduino-based PI control loop with a 1500W heater and high-temperature pump
  • CFD-optimized internal flow using Siemens NX for uniform temperature distribution
  • Modular aluminum frame with in-house fabricated stainless steel panels
  • Intuitive user interface with 3.5” display and four-button control
  • Custom sealing strategy using bonded seals, gaskets, and specialty fittings

Results & Takeaways

  • Achieved ±0.1°C steady-state accuracy
  • Delivered a fully functional, lab-ready system under $1,200
  • Selected as the top design out of 20+ teams in the 2025 Capstone Showcase

This project strengthened my capabilities in thermal system design, CFD modeling, embedded control, and hands-on fabrication — highlighting the value of user-centered thinking and creative problem-solving under constraints.

Heat Transfer Coefficient Test Rig - Accent Refrigeration Systems Ltd.

Background

This project was completed in collaboration with Accent Refrigeration to support design optimization of their snow-melting coil systems used in ice arena snow pits. The team was tasked with developing a test rig capable of measuring the heat transfer performance of submerged copper coil heat exchangers under real-world conditions.

Objectives

  • Provide standardized, repeatable testing for multiple coil designs
  • Operate within 10–35°C heated fluid range and 0–ambient°C bath conditions
  • Measure flow, pressure, and temperature with ±0.5°C accuracy
  • Enable data-driven design improvements for future coil iterations

Engineering Approach

  • Designed a closed-loop fluid circuit with a 40 kW immersion heater and a 1 hp pump circulating glycol solution through nine submerged copper coils
  • Integrated sensors for temperature, pressure, flow, ambient air, and bath level
  • Included a bottom-mounted agitator to maintain uniform bath mixing
  • Delivered full documentation: P&ID, one-line electrical diagram, CAD layout, and detailed component sizing
  • Heat transfer modeled as a resistance network, with performance estimated using LMTD and Dittus–Boelter equations

Results & Takeaways

  • Achieved a feasible and costed system design with a total estimated budget of $18,439 CAD
  • Analytical plots confirmed that increased flow rates significantly improve heat transfer, while agitator effects were less pronounced
  • Delivered a comprehensive build package ready for prototype development and experimental validation

This project strengthened my understanding of system-level design, instrumentation, and technical documentation. It highlighted the value of working closely with industry clients and balancing theoretical design with real-world application.

CFD/FEA Bike Frame Optimization

Background

This project aimed to aerodynamically and structurally optimize the frame of an Apollo Volare performance road bike using advanced FEA and CFD techniques. Targeted at competitive cyclists, the design focused on reducing aerodynamic drag while ensuring structural reliability under realistic loading, including crosswind exposure.

Objectives

  • Reduce aerodynamic drag using CFD-guided geometry changes
  • Maintain structural integrity under rider weight via static FEA
  • Validate performance across crosswind angles (0°–20°)
  • Preserve OEM compatibility while enabling high-performance upgrades and manufacturability

Engineering Approach

  • Conducted baseline FEA and CFD using mesh convergence to assess stress concentrations under distributed loads
  • Performed steady and transient CFD simulations at 40 km/h with 4 wind angles using a Realizable k-ε turbulence model
  • Iteratively transitioned from circular to elliptical (4.5:1) and teardrop profiles to reduce flow separation and wake drag
  • Performed crosswind analysis at yaw angles of 10°, 15°, and 20°
  • Optimized mesh refinement around high-gradient regions to balance simulation fidelity and runtime

Results & Takeaways

  • Achieved a 31% reduction in drag coefficient (1.4 → 0.97) through aerodynamic re-shaping
  • Final design retained low frame stress levels well below yield, despite thinner cross-section
  • Crosswind testing showed up to 50% reduction in drag force compared to the baseline under 10–20° yaw angles

This project sharpened my ability to integrate FEA and CFD for real-world optimization. It reinforced the importance of turbulence modeling, mesh strategy, and geometry iteration in pushing performance without compromising structural integrity.

Mold Design & Manufacturing

Background

This project focused on designing and machining a mold for a custom bike pedal using HDPE blocks and CNC milling. The design featured complex 3D geometry, including curved surfaces, pockets, holes, and protrusions. The part was modeled with mirror symmetry, allowing two identical molds to create the full geometry for injection molding.

Objectives

  • Apply DFM principles to develop a mold suitable for 3-axis CNC milling
  • Generate and compare multiple CAM toolpaths to balance surface quality and machining time
  • Validate toolpaths using VERICUT simulation to eliminate gouging and material excess
  • Execute end-to-end manufacturing under real lab time constraints using a Haas CNC machine

Engineering Approach

  • Created four NC programs using Mastercam with varied finishing strategies: Scallop, Hybrid, Raster, and Equal Scallop
  • Used ½", ¼", and ⅛" tools for facing, roughing, finishing, and drilling operations
  • Simulated all paths in VERICUT, analyzing tool deflection, gouges, and excess material
  • Selected optimal programs based on simulation feedback, surface quality, and machining runtime

Results & Takeaways

  • Program #1 (Scallop finish, pre-drilled holes) produced the smoothest, most accurate mold with minimal excess and no gouging
  • Program #4 (Equal scallop finish, post-drilled holes) resulted in rougher surfaces and reduced cut accuracy due to missed geometry and angled drilling
  • Completed machining of both molds within a 90-minute shop floor window, including tool changes and setup

This project provided hands-on experience in toolpath optimization, CAM verification, and precision machining. It demonstrated the critical link between simulation accuracy and real-world output, reinforcing our ability to engineer for quality, efficiency, and manufacturability under time pressure.

HVAC Design & Energy Modeling

Background

This project focused on designing an energy-efficient mechanical system for a convenience store within a proposed net-zero village in Washington’s Olympic Peninsula. The building was to be integrated into a district-wide geothermal system, emphasizing passive envelope strategies and low-emission HVAC solutions.

Objectives

  • Minimize energy consumption through envelope optimization and infiltration control
  • Design a complete HVAC system including coil sizing, ductwork, and diffuser layout
  • Integrate with a geothermal hydronic network for sustainable heating
  • Validate energy performance using eQUEST simulation and hand-calculated loads

Engineering Approach

  • Developed a full HVAC spec including airflows, diffuser sizing, duct layout, and fan selection
  • Performed detailed psychrometric and load calculations under ASHRAE 62.1 guidelines
  • Simulated annual energy use in eQUEST, adjusting design for both performance and feasibility
  • Cross-checked simulation output against hand-calculated results for validation

Results & Takeaways

  • Achieved a 60% reduction in heating and cooling demand compared to the unoptimized design, significantly lowering system loads
  • Engineered a full HVAC solution delivering 8377 L/s airflow and 43.9 kW / 35.2 kW heating/cooling capacity, fully compatible with geothermal infrastructure
  • eQUEST simulations confirmed strong alignment with hand calculations, validating both load assumptions and equipment sizing

This project developed my ability to engineer HVAC systems from the ground up—balancing thermal performance, system efficiency, and real-world constraints. It also reinforced how early design decisions around envelope and air distribution have lasting impacts on energy use and mechanical sizing.

Hybrid Tilt-Wing VTOL for Wildfire Response

Background

This project involved the conceptual design of a hybrid-electric tilt-wing VTOL aircraft to support the BC Wildfire Service (BCWS) in rapid evacuation and emergency response across wildfire-prone terrain. The design was tailored to meet BCWS's specific range and payload needs, offering vertical takeoff and landing capability, efficient fixed-wing cruise, and significantly reduced emissions, thereby directly addressing current gaps in the market.

Objectives

  • Transport 8 passengers over a 250 km range at 10,000 ft cruise altitude, aligned with wildfire response airspace constraints
  • Integrate a hybrid-electric powertrain to reduce emissions and engine wear during smoke-intensive missions
  • Achieve positive static and dynamic stability across all flight phases, including crosswind and hover conditions
  • Minimize noise (<85 dB SPL) and ensure compatibility with rooftop and remote helipads (≤11 m wingspan)

Engineering Approach

  • Designed a tilt-wing configuration for smoother transitions and reduced mechanical complexity
  • Integrated a hybrid powertrain: 1 MW turbogenerator, Ardiden 1U turboshaft, four Magni500 electric motors, and Li-S battery packs
  • Performed aerodynamic optimization using XFLR5 with NACA 23018/0015 profiles
  • Optimized internal component placement and CG through iterative CAD and stability analysis

Results & Takeaways

  • Achieved a maximum takeoff mass of 2733 kg, with 31% lower MTOM per passenger than comparable tilt-wing platforms
  • Verified longitudinal, lateral, and directional stability, with a 17.6% static margin
  • Hybrid-electric architecture enabled smoke-safe VTOL operation, with up to 866 kg CO₂ saved per flight when flying in full-electric mode

This project showcased our ability to lead a complete concept-to-simulation aircraft design, integrating propulsion, aerodynamics, structural layout, and environmental impact. It strengthened our systems engineering mindset—balancing innovation with feasibility, mission risk, and regulatory constraints.
